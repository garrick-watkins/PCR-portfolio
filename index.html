Final Reflection — Professional Development at WWU

My path to a Bachelor of Arts in Business Analytics has not been a straight line, and I think that is the most valuable thing about it. I started at the University of Washington in pre-science, earned recognition on the Dean's List, and discovered along the way that my real interests sat at the intersection of data, decision-making, and the operational realities of how organizations actually run. I am heading into the supply chain and logistics industry with a specific focus on AI integration, a field where the central challenge is augmenting human decision-making with predictive intelligence while keeping people accountable for the calls that matter most. My background uniquely prepares me for this work: I have practiced making decisions under uncertainty with imperfect information, I understand operations as a coordinated movement of people, equipment, and information, and my analytics training has given me the tools to model and improve those systems at scale. Western Washington University has been the place where these pieces came together into a coherent professional identity, and I am leaving here ready to bring an unusual but valuable combination of operational realism and analytical rigor to whatever team I join next.

Class That Counts

MIS 321 — Systems Analysis and Design 3 PTS

Spring Quarter 2025 · Field project completed May 23, 2025

In MIS 321, I worked with a three-person team to conduct a real-world Systems Analysis Field Project for Quicksilver Photo Lab, a photography business in downtown Bellingham owned by Cate Reed. Our team — Kennedy Harris, Kai Borgnino, and myself — operated as a consulting group called IS DZign Consultants, conducting an in-person introductory meeting, a two-hour stakeholder interview, and ongoing email correspondence to gather the information needed to analyze their Sales/Return Transactions and Reporting System. I contributed to mapping their current system through Context and Level-0 Data Flow Diagrams, assessing how their Clover POS, QuickBooks, and Trello tools actually performed in practice, and developing professional recommendations for system enhancements like digitized order forms and real-time transaction tracking. What I valued most about this project was that we were responsible for a real client's information and operations — including identifying concerns about how customer data was being stored — which made professional standards feel concrete rather than abstract. Our team also maintained a formal log of every interaction and sent a thank-you note after the engagement, both of which made me realize how much professionalism shows up in the small habits, not just the deliverables. The course pushed me to take ownership of work that an actual small business owner would read and rely on, which is a different kind of accountability than turning in a problem set. I will apply this experience directly as a business analyst, where I will routinely be in the position of analyzing real systems, surfacing concerns to stakeholders, and producing documentation that other people will act on. MIS 321 was where I learned that professionalism in technical work is fundamentally about respecting the people who depend on your analysis.

[ 02 ] Accessibility, Equity, Diversity & Inclusion

03 PTS
Class That Counts

MGMT 311 — Introduction to Management and Organizational Behavior 3 PTS

Spring Quarter 2025 · Completed June 2025

MGMT 311 explored organization theory, interpersonal communication, power dynamics, and the role of values and philosophy within organizations — topics that sit at the intersection of management and equity, diversity, and inclusion. The capstone group project, Mighty Management, was a deep dive into the managerial practices of Amy Imhof-Harris, the Nurse Manager of Interventional Radiology at Tacoma General Hospital, who leads a team of about 30 nurses and technologists. Working with a four-person team of students from different backgrounds, I helped conduct a recorded interview, cross-reference our notes for accuracy, and develop recommendations grounded in management theory. Our final recommendation emphasized smaller-group meetings to promote psychological safety, more inclusive communication channels, and giving employees stronger voice through structured feedback — concepts that pushed me to see inclusion as an operational practice, not just a value statement. What I appreciated most was learning that inclusion is something a manager builds through everyday choices like meeting structure, feedback format, and which suggestions actually get implemented. The course also gave me vocabulary for things I had already experienced as a wildland firefighter — how team voice gets heard or lost, and how individual autonomy connects to motivation in high-pressure environments. As I move into business analytics roles, I will apply these lessons by being deliberate about whose voice is in the room during decision-making and how data findings get communicated back to the people that data describes. Inclusive management is not a side concern in analytics work — it is how you make sure analysis actually serves the whole team, not just whoever is loudest in the meeting.

Professional Experience

Wildland Firefighter — Washington Department of Natural Resources 3 PTS

June 2025 — September 2025

During the summer between my junior and senior years at WWU, I worked as a wildland firefighter for the Washington Department of Natural Resources, serving as Incident Commander on 15-person teams responding to active fires across the state. The role required making real-time tactical decisions under conditions where information was incomplete, situations evolved minute-by-minute, and the cost of a wrong call was measured in safety, equipment, and acres lost — essentially a live operations management problem. I also trained a first-year firefighter on safety protocols, equipment use, and field decision-making, which gave me direct experience onboarding someone into a complex operational system, and I coordinated with multiple agencies and aerial units to translate ground conditions into clear, concise updates that leadership could act on. What I valued most was learning that effective leadership in chaotic environments depends on simple, repeatable communication frameworks and trust built through preparation, not improvisation. The experience also made me realize that wildland fire response is fundamentally a supply chain and logistics operation: deploying crews, equipment, water, and information to the right place at the right time, across multiple coordinating agencies, under constantly shifting conditions. This perspective is directly relevant to my career goal of working in AI-integrated supply chain and logistics, where the core challenge is augmenting human decisions with predictive data while keeping a human in the loop for the calls that matter most. I have already practiced the mindset that this industry needs — trusting inputs from weather services, fire behavior models, and aerial reconnaissance to inform my decisions, while remaining accountable for the final call on the ground. As I move into supply chain and AI roles after graduation, I will bring an operational realism that most analysts coming straight from coursework do not have, and I will be able to design and use AI tools with a clear sense of what actually happens when systems get tested under real-world conditions.
